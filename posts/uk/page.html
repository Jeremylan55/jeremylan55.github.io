Uken Games

Overview

During Winter 2017 I worked at "Uken Games". The studio located in Toronto which develops evergreen game title such as "Bingo Pop", "Kings of Pool" and "Jeopardy" with more than 5 million active players worldwide.

My role as a UI UX designer was to craft the smoothest playing experience, drive retention and monetization. I got the opportunity to design large scale A/B tests with pools containing up to a hundred thousand players. I also coordinated w ith the artists in the creation of optimized art assets to keep the game as efficient as possible.

Work Flow

The typical feature workflow at Uken initially start as a one-pager summarizing the goals, risk and OKRs then moves to design phase then into the "Epic" phase.

Workflow at Uken

The design phase usually kicks off with a meeting with the product team where we would brainstorm the user outcomes and possible high-level user flows. Below are pictures of one of the many brainstorming session I had at Uken. This exercise makes sure that we did not leave any possibility unexplored.

Mocking up Features

Once the user flows are planned and the wireframes are made, I would move to start creating the high fidelity mocks. It is crucial to follow the art direction set in the game to maintain user immersion which is crucial to retention. It is also very important to create a UI which obeys a consistent set of rules and mimic real life interactions to not confuse the player. For example, everything in the game is layered and has volume.

Monetization

I designed a framework that let Uken to dynamically change LTOS. This gave the company the ability to perform large-scale A/B testing with hundred of thousand of players. This enable us to determine when/where our player based would be more susceptible to purchase and item and the triggers involved. Below are some of the LTO I illustrated for them. Each of them trying to tackle a different trigger either being negative or positive. For example, the first one focus on a positive trigger and the scarcity aspect of the item on sales, the second one focus on a negative trigger and the third one focuses on "Loss aversion" with its very short timer.

Improving core controls

I went through hundreds of user feedback to determine their frustrations. This helped us in many aspects improving the core controls. The interesting dilemma was to find the right balance between the least cognitive load required and how challenging can the gameplay be.
